ABOUT

Research profile

Dr. Paulo Roberto de Lima Bittencourt is a tropical plant ecologist at Cardiff University whose research focuses on understanding how plants and water shape the ecology, evolution and functioning of tropical environments. His work combines plant ecophysiology, tropical forest ecology and the development of sensor technologies to investigate how trees function under changing environmental conditions.

His research is centered on the plant water transport system, the hydraulic network that supports leaves mechanically, maintains hydration and supplies nutrients. This system sets the functional limits of plants and strongly influences their ecology, evolution and responses to climate change.

Within GigaFlor and PELD-MIAG, Dr. Bittencourt contributes mechanistic expertise on tropical tree function, plant hydraulics, drought sensitivity, canopy function, biomechanical resistance and the physiological processes that regulate the performance and vulnerability of giant tropical trees.

SCIENTIFIC APPROACH

Plant hydraulics, sensors and tropical forest function

Dr. Bittencourt’s work investigates how the water transport system of tropical plants modulates processes from nano-scale physiology to ecosystem ecology and large-scale biogeochemistry. His research links plant structure and function to broader questions about carbon cycling, rainfall recycling, drought vulnerability and the future of tropical forests under climate change.

He has been involved in major tropical forest experiments designed to understand how tropical trees respond to changes in water, carbon dioxide and nutrient availability. These efforts include work in large-scale experimental platforms such as AmazonFACE and the Caxiuanã Throughfall Exclusion Experiment, where plant hydraulic systems are central to interpreting tree responses to environmental stress.

A distinctive component of his scientific contribution is the development and application of new monitoring technologies for tropical ecology. His work integrates ecological theory, field-based measurements, electronic sensor development and long-term monitoring to overcome limitations in observing water, carbon and biomechanical processes in tropical trees.

GIGAFLOR CONTRIBUTION

Mechanistic understanding of giant tropical forests

Dr. Bittencourt also works on giant tropical forests, ecosystems with high densities of large trees, including individuals exceeding 80 m in height. These forests store exceptional amounts of biomass and harbor biodiversity that remains insufficiently explored.

His contribution to GigaFlor strengthens the mechanistic dimension of the observatory by connecting forest inventory, dendrometric monitoring, functional ecology, plant water transport and climate-change vulnerability. His expertise helps address central questions of the project: why giant forests occur in specific tropical landscapes, how giant trees function and transport water, and how sensitive they are to drought and climate change.

By integrating field ecology, plant physiology and sensor-based monitoring, his work supports the development of predictive frameworks for understanding the fate of giant tropical trees and their implications for terrestrial carbon stores, climate regulation and ecosystem resilience.

FIELDWORK AND TECHNOLOGY

From tropical fieldwork to next-generation monitoring

Dr. Bittencourt is strongly committed to tropical fieldwork and to the value of collaborative research across diverse cultural, institutional and scientific contexts. His work brings together riverine communities, students, academics, innovators and decision-makers to strengthen tropical forest science and conservation.

His approach is especially relevant to GigaFlor because monitoring giant Amazonian trees requires new technologies capable of measuring growth, water transport, carbon dynamics and biomechanical processes under challenging tropical field conditions.
